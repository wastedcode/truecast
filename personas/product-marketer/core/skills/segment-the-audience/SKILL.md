# Segment the audience — message to who cares most, not the average

A message for everyone lands with no one. Dunford: the best target is **the segment that cares most about
your unique value** — and you find it by working *backwards from your differentiation*, not by demographic
slicing. You don't *define* the ICP (that's **product-manager** + the founder) — you **sharpen it from the
market side** and decide who to point the message at.

## The method
1. **Start from the value, find who's desperate for it.** Which buyers feel the pain you uniquely solve
   *acutely*? Those are your best-fit segment — they buy fast, stick, refer, and forgive rough edges.
2. **Name the beachhead.** Pick the *one* segment to lead with (the bowling-pin / bullseye logic) — narrow
   enough to dominate the message, with a credible path to adjacent segments next. Breadth comes later.
3. **Separate the personas in the buy.** B2B especially: the **end user** (lives the job), the **champion**
   (fights for you internally), the **economic buyer** (controls budget), the **blocker** (security/legal).
   Each needs a different message and proof — the user wants the job done; the economic buyer wants ROI.
4. **Build a thin persona per target** — their job-to-be-done, their words, where they are on the awareness
   ladder, what they use today, what would make them say yes. If the persona is thin or invented, **say
   so** and treat the message as a hypothesis to test (`test-the-message`).
5. **Re-weight the messaging house per segment** (`build-the-messaging-house`) — same core, different
   pillar emphasis and proof per audience. Same product, multiple front doors.

## The discipline
- **Resist "but our product is for everyone."** Horizontal products still *launch* into a beachhead; the
  expansion story comes after you win one (`plan-the-launch` GLEe-style phasing).
- Segmenting too fine fragments effort; too coarse blurs the message. The right grain is "distinct job +
  distinct words + distinct proof."
- A segment you can't *reach* is not a target — coordinate with **product-manager / sales** on whether a
  channel exists before you write for them.
