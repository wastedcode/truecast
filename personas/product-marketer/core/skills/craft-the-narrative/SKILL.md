# Craft the narrative — the story with stakes

A feature list informs; a narrative *moves*. Use Andy Raskin's strategic-narrative arc (*"The Greatest
Sales Deck I've Ever Seen"*) — one story that powers sales, the launch, the homepage, and the pitch alike.
The product is never the hero; the **customer reaching the Promised Land** is.

## The arc
1. **Name the change in the world** (the why-now). Lead with the **undeniable shift** that creates stakes
   and urgency — *not* "you have a problem" (that puts the prospect on the defensive). The change is true
   whether or not they buy; it earns the right to the rest.
2. **Show winners and losers.** The change splits the world — those who adapt win, those who don't fall
   behind. Raise the stakes so standing still feels risky.
3. **Tease the Promised Land** — a desirable, hard-to-reach **future state**, defined in the customer's
   terms, *not* your product. This is the implicit promise: go with us and you get here.
4. **Introduce features as "magic gifts"** for slaying the obstacles between them and the Promised Land —
   each capability earns its place by removing a specific obstacle, in order.
5. **Prove you can deliver** — evidence, ideally a **success story of someone like them** who already
   reached the Promised Land. Concrete beats abstract every time.

## The discipline
- **Promised Land ≠ product.** If you can't describe the future state without naming your features, you
  have a brochure, not a narrative. Write the destination first, the gifts second.
- **One narrative, many surfaces.** The same arc compresses to a headline, expands to a keynote, and
  underlies the messaging house (`build-the-messaging-house`). Don't run three contradictory stories.
- **The change must be one the customer already half-believes** (Schwartz: channel existing desire, don't
  manufacture it). Naming a shift they feel but haven't articulated is the strongest possible hook.
- **Stakes without hype.** Make standing still feel costly using *their* reality, not manufactured fear.
- Tie it to the positioning (`write-the-positioning`): the change implies the category; the Promised Land
  implies the value; the gifts are the unique attributes.
