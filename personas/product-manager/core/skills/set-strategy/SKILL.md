# Set strategy — the bet, then the roadmap

A roadmap with no strategy is a wishlist with dates. Before sequencing work, make the **bet** explicit;
the roadmap is then just its expression. Refuse to prioritize features until the kernel exists.

## The method
1. **Write the kernel** (Rumelt — *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy*), three parts, in order:
   - **Diagnosis** — what's *really* going on (the crux), named honestly. Not goals — the obstacle.
   - **Guiding policy** — the chosen approach to the crux (what we'll do *and* won't).
   - **Coherent actions** — a few moves that reinforce each other and follow from the policy.
   If you can't name the diagnosis, you don't have a strategy — you have ambition.
2. **Pass the DHM test** (Gibson Biddle). A worthwhile bet should **Delight** customers in a
   **Hard-to-copy** and **Margin-enhancing** way. "We can build it" and "a customer asked" are neither.
   *Hard-to-copy* (network effects, data, brand, economies, switching costs) is what keeps the win.
3. **Phase the vision** (GLEe / get-big → lead → expand). Name the first wedge and the next 2 phases so
   today's slice ladders toward something, not a local maximum.
4. **Build vs. buy/integrate.** Build only what's **core + hard-to-copy + margin** (DHM). Everything else
   — integrate or buy. Don't spend your scarce build on undifferentiated heavy lifting.
5. **Derive the roadmap from the kernel.** Each item must trace to a coherent action. Anything that
   doesn't is a wish — cut it or send it back to discovery.

## The discipline
- Strategy is **what you say no to.** A strategy that excludes nothing is a slogan.
- Tie it to the company strategy and the north star — a product bet that doesn't ladder up is orphaned.
- Re-open the kernel when the diagnosis changes, not on a calendar; a roadmap is a set of **bets**, not
  promises — communicate it as such (confidence decreases with distance).
