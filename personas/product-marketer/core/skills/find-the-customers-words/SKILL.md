# Find the customer's words — voice-of-customer, then write

The strongest copy isn't invented — it's **overheard**. *"Join the conversation already happening in the
customer's head"* (Robert Collier). Before writing, harvest the customer's **literal language**: the words
they use for the problem, the outcome, and the alternatives — and the words they *don't* use. Then write
in *their* register, not yours. This is the antidote to "generic marketing soup" and the curse of
knowledge.

## Where to mine (voice-of-customer sources)
- **Sales/support calls and transcripts** — how they describe the pain in the moment, unprompted.
- **Reviews and forums** — yours and competitors'. The "I switched because…" and "I wish it could…" lines
  are gold; competitor 1-star reviews are a differentiation map.
- **Interviews** — ask about the last time they hit the problem; capture exact phrasing (Mom-Test logic:
  past behavior and stories, not opinions on your copy).
- **Search queries / community questions** — the words they type when nobody's selling to them.
- **Existing shipped copy + the founder's own messages** — the voice already has a shape (`honor-the-voice`).

## The method
1. **Collect raw phrases verbatim** — don't paraphrase into marketing-speak; the power is in the exact
   words ("it's a nightmare at month-end," not "users experience friction during reconciliation").
2. **Cluster by job and by emotion** — group the language around the functional / social / emotional jobs
   (`message-the-job-not-the-feature`). Note recurring metaphors and the words that carry feeling.
3. **Steal the headline.** The best headline is often a customer sentence lightly polished. Test it
   (`test-the-message`) — does the *target* segment say "yes, that's me"?
4. **Build a living words bank** — the register (plain vs. playful, technical vs. lay), the words they use,
   the words they reject, the hooks that read true. Grow it every time you write.

## The discipline
- **Use their words, not yours.** A technical audience wants precise terms; a lay audience wants plain
  language; mismatch on either side breaks trust. Match the audience, not the team's habit.
- **If the persona/insight is thin, say so** — and treat the language as a hypothesis to validate, not
  fact. Don't fabricate a "customer voice" from imagination; that's the soup you're trying to avoid.
