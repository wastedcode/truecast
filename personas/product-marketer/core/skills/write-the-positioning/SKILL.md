# Write the positioning — the single source all copy derives from

Positioning is **context-setting** — *"what you do to the mind of the prospect"* (Ries & Trout) — and the
one document every artifact derives from. Write it once; don't re-invent it per page. Dunford:
*"positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something a defined market cares
a lot about."* Customers **use what they know to figure out what they don't** — so positioning frames the
unfamiliar in familiar terms.

## The method (Dunford's 5+1 components — in causal order)
1. **Competitive alternatives** — if you didn't exist, what would they *actually* do? (Often a
   spreadsheet, a manual process, the intern, or "nothing.") This — not a feature comparison — is the
   real frame the customer judges you against. Get this wrong and everything downstream is wrong.
2. **Unique attributes** — what you have that the alternatives don't (capabilities, not adjectives).
3. **Value (+ proof)** — the benefit those attributes enable that the customer *cares a lot about*. Each
   value must trace to an attribute, and each attribute must earn its value. Drop attributes with no value.
4. **Target who cares most** — the segment for whom that value is acute, not "everyone." The best target
   is the one who buys fast, sticks, and tells others (`segment-the-audience`).
5. **Market category** — the frame that makes the value obvious. "Customers use what they know…" — pick
   the category that triggers the right associations and sets you up to win, not a default you inherited.
6. **(+1) Relevant trends** — ride a trend customers already believe in *only if* it's true for you; a
   bolted-on trend ("now with AI!") reads as hype and erodes trust.

## The discipline
- **Truth is a gate, not a preference.** Every claim must trace to a *shipped* capability. For a technical
  audience a claim the product can't back reads as a lie and is a correctness bug (`honor-the-voice`).
- **Pre-users, positioning is a hypothesis** — built from analogy, competitor language, and founder
  intuition. Label it as such; test it on the first real prospects (`test-the-message`). Don't let a
  hypothesis pose as validated.
- **The category choice is the highest-leverage move.** Entering an established category means you're
  compared on its terms; creating/reframing one means you teach the terms — harder, but it can make a
  crowded comparison disappear. Choose deliberately.
- Output is a short doc (the five components + the one-liner it implies). Everything else — narrative,
  messaging house, copy — derives from it. If two artifacts disagree, the positioning doc is the referee.
