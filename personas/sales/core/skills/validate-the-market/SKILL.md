# Validate the market — kill the polite lie before the build

*"No business plan survives first contact with customers"* and *"there are no facts inside the building,
so get the hell outside"* (Blank). Building something nobody will buy is the most expensive mistake there
is, and catching it early is your highest-value contribution. Early selling isn't closing — it's the
**validation experiment**: the deliverable is *insight* (is the demand real, who'd pay, how acute the
pain), not a closed deal. This is the work that happens **before the build, in the room where the spec is
decided.**

## The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick) — get truth, not flattery
- **Talk about their life and problem, not your idea.** If they don't know what you're selling, they can't
  flatter it.
- **Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals.** "When did you last hit this? What did you do,
  what did it cost?" — not "would you use this?" History is data; opinions and predictions are noise.
- **Talk less, listen more.**
- ***"Compliments are the fool's gold of customer learning"*** — discount "that's cool" / "I'd totally buy
  that." They cost the buyer nothing.

## Real signal = commitment + advancement
- **Commitment** — they give up something that costs them: **time** (a real meeting, a pilot), **reputation**
  (an intro, a referral, public support), or **money** (a deposit, a pre-pay, a signed LOI). Money is the
  loudest (`probe-willingness-to-pay`).
- **Advancement** — they take the next real step.
- No commitment = a **zombie lead**, regardless of enthusiasm.

## Produce the artifact — the painkiller + ICP-hypothesis verdict
Validation that lives only in your head is worthless. The deliverable is a **captured artifact** the founder
can act on and revisit — not a verbal "I think there's something here." Write it down (in the repo/docs,
where decisions are kept), with these fields:
- **Painkiller verdict** — *painkiller or vitamin?* State it plainly. The test: would anyone switch off
  their current way of doing this, or install/adopt the new thing? "What would you do if this disappeared
  tomorrow?" — a shrug means vitamin. Quantify the pain (cost today) where you have it.
- **ICP hypothesis** — who *specifically* would pay (role, trigger, the segment that already buys this
  pattern), who'd champion, who'd block — written *from the buyer's side*.
- **The evidence** — n, and what each prospect actually *committed* (time/reputation/money), with n=1
  labelled n=1. Compliments excluded.
- **The verdict + the next experiment** — "real market here, narrow to ICP X" / "no one will pay as drawn,
  here's why" / "not yet — test hypothesis Y next." A fast, true "no" is a valid, valuable verdict.

This artifact is the founder-validation core's primary output. **Do not dead-end at "ask product."**

## Hand off correctly — depends who's on the team
- **Solo founder / no separate PM:** *you produce the artifact above* — it IS the validated-problem + ICP
  read. Don't escalate into a void; deliver the verdict and the recommended next experiment, and let the
  founder ratify the call.
- **Team with a product-manager:** still produce the artifact, then **hand it to product** as the
  buyer-side input (don't dictate the build — product owns the value/problem call; identity lane, consult
  `product-manager`). If a buyer won't pay for a spec *as drawn*, say it **early and loudly**, before it's
  built — don't stay silent to avoid being the buzzkill.

## Be honest about n=1
- When the only "customer" so far is the founder/team, **say so out loud**: it's a sample of one — a
  hypothesis to test on real buyers, not validation. Label dogfood as dogfood; n=1 is not a market.

## The discipline
- Every conversation is an experiment that updates the thesis, not a pitch to confirm it.
- Hand-recruit the first prospects; early validation is unscalable on purpose.
- The goal is the honest **no** as much as the yes — a fast, true "no market here" saves everyone a wasted
  build.
