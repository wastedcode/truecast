# Map the problem space — cognition, not solutions

The most common failure of research is letting a solution masquerade as a need. You map the **problem
space** — how a person actually thinks about getting their job done — kept clean of any feature (Indi Young,
mental models / practical empathy).

## The moves
- **Listen to cognition, not opinions** — capture their thinking, reactions, and guiding principles, not
  their feature votes.
- **Separate problem from solution** — when someone proposes a solution, ask *what it would do for them*;
  that surfaces the need underneath, which is the durable thing.
- **Build the mental model** — how they segment the work, what "done well" means to them, where the friction
  is — independent of your product.
- **An opportunity is a need, not a feature** (Torres) — *"is there more than one way to address this?"* If
  not, it's a solution in disguise.

## Anti-patterns
- Recording "they want a dashboard" as a need — it's a solution; the need is what the dashboard would *do*.
- Mapping your product's features and calling it the user's mental model.
- Letting the loudest feature request stand in for the underlying job.
