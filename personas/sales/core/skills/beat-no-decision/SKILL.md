# Beat no-decision — the biggest loss in modern B2B

**40–60% of qualified pipeline dies to "no decision"** — not to a competitor (Dixon & McKenna, *The JOLT
Effect*, from 2.5M sales calls). And the counterintuitive finding: **56% of those losses are
indecision** — the buyer's *fear of making the wrong choice* (omission bias) — only 44% are status-quo
preference. This matters enormously because the two need **opposite** treatments:
- **Status-quo bias** (they don't see why to change) → create urgency, sharpen the cost of inaction.
- **Indecision / fear** (they want to change but are afraid to commit) → **urgency makes it WORSE.**
  Pushing a scared buyer raises the stakes of a mistake and pushes them to "no decision." You must
  **de-risk**, not pressure.

So first **diagnose which one you're facing** (`handle-the-objection` helps), then act.

## JOLT — overcoming indecision
1. **J — Judge the indecision.** Read the signals and find the *source*: a **valuation** problem (can't
   choose between options), an **information** problem (keeps asking for more), or **outcome uncertainty**
   (afraid it won't work). Asking for endless info is a fear signal, not a logic gap — don't feed it.
2. **O — Offer a recommendation.** Stop presenting options neutrally and **tell them what you'd do** —
   confidently, as the expert: "For a team your size, I'd start with the X tier." Buyers want guidance,
   not a buffet.
3. **L — Limit the exploration.** Narrow the options; cap the "let me also evaluate five more vendors"
   spiral. More choices deepen paralysis. Proactively answer the questions *before* they ask, so they stop
   hunting for the one fact that makes it safe.
4. **T — Take risk off the table.** De-risk the decision: a pilot, a phased rollout, a money-back/opt-out,
   a success guarantee, a smaller first step, references who survived the same fear. Make the *downside*
   small so the *yes* is easy.

## The discipline
- **Stop saying "what's the urgency?" to a scared buyer.** It's the most common mistake and it backfires.
- "Let me think about it" is rarely a real schedule — it's unresolved fear. Surface it: "What's the part
  you're not sure about?" then de-risk *that*.
- JOLT applies only to a **qualified** deal (real metric, EB, champion). An unqualified "stall" is just a
  zombie — disqualify it (`qualify-the-deal`), don't JOLT it.
- The honest version of de-risking is real (a true pilot, a real guarantee) — never a fake safety net to
  trick a yes. You sell honest value (identity).
