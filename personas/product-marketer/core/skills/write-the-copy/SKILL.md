# Write the copy — the last mile from message to words that land

Positioning, the narrative, and the messaging house decide *what* to say. This is the craft of **how it
reads on the page** — the part that decides whether the aha actually fires. A page can be strategically
correct and still flat, vague, or skimmed-past. Most copy dies here, not in the strategy: the message was
right and the execution buried it. *"The copy isn't the strategy. The copy is where the strategy survives
or dies"* — Emma Stratton (Punchy). Strategy is a question of clarity of thought; copy is a question of
clarity on the page, and they fail independently.

## The craft (per artifact)
1. **Win the first line, or you don't get the second.** The hook/headline carries 80% of the weight —
   readers scan, they don't read. Lead with the **single most relevant outcome** for this segment at this
   awareness stage (`sequence-by-awareness`), in the customer's own words (`find-the-customers-words`).
   Not the company, not the feature — the reader's payoff. If the headline only works *with* the
   sub-headline, it's not a headline yet.
2. **One idea per unit.** One idea per headline, per section, per sentence. Copy that tries to say three
   things says none. Cut every sentence to the one thing it must do; if two ideas fight, split them or kill
   one.
3. **Concrete beats clever, specific beats vague.** "Save 20 minutes a day" beats "boost productivity."
   Numbers, nouns, and verbs beat adjectives. Every abstraction ("seamless," "powerful," "intuitive") is a
   place you failed to be specific — replace it with the provable specific or cut it (`honor-the-voice`).
   Clever that obscures is worse than plain that lands.
4. **Write for the scan, then the read.** Real readers skim headers, bold, and the first words of each
   line. Make the page *legible at a glance*: front-load the value in every header, use short paragraphs,
   make the structure carry the argument so a skimmer who reads only the headers still gets the gist.
5. **Structure the page as an argument, not a brochure.** A landing page has a job-to-be-done shape:
   **hook → the problem/stakes → the promised land → how (the magic gifts) → proof → the one CTA.** Each
   block earns the next scroll. Classic frames are tools, not templates — **PAS** (problem-agitate-solve)
   for problem-aware readers, **the narrative arc** (`craft-the-narrative`) for a vision page, a tight
   value-prop + proof + CTA for the most-aware. Pick the frame for the awareness stage.
6. **One page, one CTA, one next action.** Decide the single action you want and make it unmissable and
   low-friction. Competing CTAs split intent; a vague CTA ("Learn more") wastes the click — say what
   happens next ("Start free — no card").
7. **Edit ruthlessly — cut by half.** First drafts are too long, too hedged, too proud of their cleverness.
   Cut throat-clearing intros, qualifiers, and any sentence the reader would skip. *"Make every word earn
   its place"* — if a word can come out without loss, it comes out. Read it aloud; if you stumble, the
   reader will too.

## Match the surface
- **Hero / homepage** — clarity over completeness; the aha in one screen. What do you do, for whom, why it
  beats the alternative — answerable above the fold.
- **Ad** — one hook, one promise, one click; the awareness stage decides whether it teases the problem or
  pitches the offer.
- **Email** — the subject line *is* the ad for the open; one idea, one ask; respect the inbox.
- **Onboarding / empty states** — copy as a guide, not decoration: reduce the next-step friction, speak to
  the job the user is mid-flow on; this is where activation (the aha) actually happens.
- **Changelog / release note** — lead with the user benefit, not the internal feature name; even Tier-3
  copy says *what it lets you now do*.

## The discipline
- **The page is where the strategy is tested, not asserted.** Draft, then put it in front of real people
  (`test-the-message`) — comprehension and resonance, not the team's taste. Beautiful copy that fails the
  5-second test is decoration.
- **Derive, don't free-write.** Every line traces to a pillar in the messaging house
  (`build-the-messaging-house`) and to a real shipped capability (`honor-the-voice`) — copy craft sharpens
  the message, it doesn't invent new claims.
- **Voice over formula.** The frames (PAS, AIDA, headline patterns) are scaffolding; strip them out of the
  final draft so it reads as the founder, not as a template (`honor-the-voice`).
