# Research the user — design from evidence, not from yourself

Design without research is design for yourself. The discipline is to learn what users actually do, think,
and need — and to pick the **right method for the question** (NN/g's method-by-question map): generative
research to discover the problem and the user, evaluative research to test a design.

## Pick the method for the question
- **"Who are they / what do they need / how do they think?"** → generative/discovery: interviews,
  contextual inquiry, diary studies, observation. Do this *before* you design.
- **"How do they group/label these concepts?"** → card sort, then tree test (`design-information-architecture`).
- **"Can they actually use what we made?"** → usability test (`usability-test`) — *not* a discovery method.
- **"How many / how often / which variant?"** → analytics, A/B (a quant question — pair with the
  product-manager's metrics; behavior data says *what*, research says *why*).

## Discovery the right way (the Mom Test discipline)
1. **Ask about their life and past behavior, not your idea.** "Walk me through the last time you did X" —
   not "would you use a feature that…?" People lie to be nice and guess badly about the future; history is
   data, hypotheticals are noise.
2. **Watch, don't just ask.** Observed behavior beats stated preference. Where they hesitate, backtrack,
   or build a workaround *is* the unmet need.
3. **Chase the struggle, not the compliment.** "That's cool" is worthless; a workaround they built, a
   tool they pay for, a time they got frustrated is gold.
4. **Capture mental models.** How does the user *think* the thing is organized / works? Design to their
   model (recognition over recall), or you create friction at every step.
5. **Separate observation from interpretation.** Write what they did; mark your inference as inference;
   don't smuggle a solution in as a finding.

## The discipline
- **WHAT to build is the product-manager's call** — if research surfaces a problem/scope question, hand
  it over; your research feeds the design, not the roadmap.
- For a tool with no external users yet, this collapses to your own observed usage + the few real
  prospects you can reach — say so; never pretend a sample of one is a market.
- One vivid observed story beats a survey of opinions, but triangulate before you commit a foundational
  surface to a single anecdote.
