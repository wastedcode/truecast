# Find the painkiller and the loop — is the pain real, and how does it spread?

Before any of the craft (positioning, narrative, copy), two questions decide whether marketing can even
work: **is this a painkiller or a vitamin?** and **why would anyone actually install / adopt it — how does
it spread?** The founder's recurring instinct is right: *"what's the painkiller here?"* and *"it's a growth
problem, not a product problem — why would anyone install it?"* No headline rescues a vitamin with no
channel. Naming this is the marketer's job, in the **positioning/demand** lane — not a polish pass.

## (a) The painkiller-vs-vitamin acuity test
A **painkiller** solves an acute, frequent, expensive pain the user is already trying to solve. A
**vitamin** is nice-to-have — good for you, easy to skip. Vitamins get downloaded and abandoned; you can
sell one for a while, but adoption leaks. Score the pain honestly on five axes:
1. **Acute?** Is it a hair-on-fire problem, or a mild annoyance they tolerate?
2. **Frequent?** Does it bite daily/weekly (a habit forms) or once a year (no muscle memory, easy to forget)?
3. **Expensive?** Does the pain cost real money, time, risk, or reputation — enough to justify the switching cost?
4. **Would they switch?** Is the value clearly *worth changing behavior for* — past the inertia of "what I do now"?
5. **What's the current workaround?** Name what they do *today* (a spreadsheet, the intern, a rival, or
   "nothing" — see `differentiate-and-counter`). The honest competitor is the workaround; you must beat it
   by enough to overcome switching cost, not just be marginally nicer.
- **The verdict:** if it's a vitamin (mild, rare, cheap-to-ignore, easy workaround), say so out loud. The
  fix may be **sharper positioning** — reframe to the acute moment / urgent segment / the painkiller use
  case buried inside it — or it may be a **product/scope finding for product-manager** (the value isn't
  acute enough yet). Don't write hype to make a vitamin sound like a painkiller; the gap gets caught and
  burns trust (`honor-the-voice`). *"Vitamins vs. painkillers"* is the canonical lens here.

## (b) The distribution / growth-loop reflex
Great product + great copy still dies if it can't *reach* and *spread*. A product with **no loop and no
channel is a growth problem, not a product problem** — and pretending more copy fixes it is the trap.
- **Name the loop, or admit there isn't one.** A growth loop is a mechanism where using the product
  *produces* the next user: invites/collaboration (you bring a teammate to use it), content/SEO (use
  creates indexable artifacts), network effects, share-the-output (the artifact carries the brand),
  word-of-mouth driven by a genuinely remarkable result. If using it doesn't help acquire the next user,
  there is no loop — say so.
- **Or name the one channel.** Per **Bullseye / Traction (Weinberg & Mares)**: brainstorm channels, pick a
  few to test cheaply, then **focus on the one channel** that actually moves the metric — don't spray. For
  an early product, naming *one* realistic channel beats a "we'll do everything" plan that does nothing.
- **The demand-side read:** if there's no loop *and* no plausible channel, that's the real blocker — flag
  it loudly and early, as a demand/distribution problem, before anyone polishes a homepage no one will see.

## The discipline & the lane
- This is **positioning + demand** craft: you diagnose *whether there's a there there* and *how it spreads*.
  You **defer the 1:1 sale and willingness-to-pay** to **sales**, and **what-to-build / scope** to
  **product-manager** — you pressure-test from the market side and escalate; you don't set the spec or run
  the deal.
- Feed the verdict forward: a real painkiller + a named loop/channel shapes `write-the-positioning` (the
  acute target + value), `plan-the-launch` (the channel/motion), and `segment-the-audience` (who feels the
  pain most). A vitamin-with-no-loop verdict is a finding to escalate, not a copy brief to accept.
- **Canon:** "vitamins vs. painkillers" (the classic acute-pain test); April Dunford (the value a defined
  market *cares a lot about*); Bullseye / *Traction* (Gabriel Weinberg & Justin Mares) — the one-channel
  focus and growth-loop thinking.
