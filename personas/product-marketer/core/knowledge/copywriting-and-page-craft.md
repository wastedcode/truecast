# Copywriting & page craft — the last mile

The execution craft behind `write-the-copy`: turning a correct message into words that land on the page.
Strategy decides *what* to say; this is *how it reads*. Read on demand.

## The core laws
- **Clarity beats cleverness.** The job is to be understood instantly, not admired. A clever line that
  needs a second read has already lost the scanner. (Emma Stratton, *Punchy* — make the message clear,
  concrete, and human; drop the jargon.)
- **Specific beats vague.** Concrete numbers, nouns, and verbs out-pull abstractions every time. Every
  adjective ("seamless," "powerful," "intuitive," "robust") is a missed chance to be specific — replace or
  cut. (Joanna Wiebe, Copyhackers — "you don't write copy, you assemble it" from the customer's words.)
- **You are joining a conversation, not starting one** (Robert Collier) — enter at the awareness stage the
  reader is already at (`sequence-by-awareness`).
- **Readers scan; they don't read.** ~80% read the headline, ~20% the body (Ogilvy). The page must work as
  a skim of headers and bolds alone.

## The headline / hook
- Carries most of the weight. Lead with the reader's **payoff**, not the company or the feature.
- Patterns (tools, not crutches): the outcome promise ("Ship in half the time"), the customer's own words
  lightly polished, the provocative truth/change ("Your spreadsheet is your real competitor"), the
  specific number ("Save 20 min a day").
- Test: does it work *alone*, without the sub-headline? Does the target say "that's me"? Any internal
  jargon = fail.

## Page structure (a landing page is an argument)
A converting page earns each scroll:
1. **Hero** — what it is, who it's for, why it beats the alternative — the aha above the fold; one CTA.
2. **Problem / stakes** — name the pain in their words; make standing still feel costly (no manufactured
   fear).
3. **Promised land** — the future state in the customer's terms, not your features (`craft-the-narrative`).
4. **How / magic gifts** — features as the way to reach it; each tied to an outcome (`message-the-job`).
5. **Proof** — metrics, customer stories, logos, screenshots; the reason-to-believe under each pillar
   (`build-the-messaging-house`).
6. **Close** — restate the value + a single, low-friction CTA.

## Classic frames (match the awareness stage)
- **PAS** — Problem · Agitate · Solve. Best for **problem-aware** readers.
- **AIDA** — Attention · Interest · Desire · Action. General-purpose funnel shape.
- **The strategic narrative** — change → stakes → promised land → gifts → proof. Best for vision/launch
  pages and decks (`craft-the-narrative`).
- **Value-prop + proof + CTA** — tight, for **most-aware** comparison shoppers.
Frames are scaffolding — strip them out of the final draft so it reads as the founder, not a template.

## Editing — cut to clarity
- **Cut by half.** First drafts are bloated. Remove throat-clearing intros, hedges ("we believe," "our
  goal is"), and qualifiers.
- **One idea per sentence; one job per section.** If a sentence does two things, split it.
- **Front-load.** Put the value in the first words of every line; skimmers read left-to-right and stop.
- **Read it aloud.** Stumbles = rewrites. Copy that's awkward to say is awkward to read.
- **Make every word earn its place** — if it can come out without loss, it comes out.

## The anti-AI-tell scrub (the slop detector)
A savvy reader clocks AI-written copy in a sentence. AI-*assisted* is fine; reads-as-AI is not. Scrub
every draft for these tells — each is an automatic rewrite (full list + rationale in `scale-with-ai-not-slop`):
- **"It's not just X — it's Y"** (and "more than a tool…") — the signature LLM tic. Kill on sight.
- **Triadic lists** — the relentless rule-of-three; break the cadence.
- **Hollow superlatives** — seamless/powerful/robust/game-changing/elevate/unlock/supercharge/leverage.
- **Em-dash tics & over-punctuation** — vary the rhythm; most em-dashes are a period.
- **Over-hedging** — "can help you", "designed to", "aims to", "may/often/typically".
- **Uniform section structure** — every block the same header+sentence+three-bullets shape.
- **Engagement scaffolding** — "in today's fast-paced world", "let's dive in", "imagine a world where",
  rhetorical-question openers, "in conclusion".
- **Generic positivity** — copy that reads true with a competitor's name pasted in says nothing.
Read it aloud: if it sounds like a press release or a chatbot, it failed. Test: *would a savvy human
marketer have written this line?*

## CTAs
- One primary action per page; competing CTAs split intent.
- Say what happens next ("Start free — no card"), not "Learn more"/"Submit."
- Reduce friction at the point of action; the copy right next to the button matters most.

## Surface-specific notes
- **Email** — the subject line is the ad for the open; one idea, one ask; the first line previews in the
  inbox, so it carries weight too.
- **Ads** — one hook, one promise, one click; the awareness stage decides tease-the-problem vs. pitch-the-
  offer.
- **Onboarding / empty states** — copy as guide; reduce next-step friction; this is where activation (the
  aha) happens, so it's high-leverage, not afterthought copy.
- **Changelog / release notes** — lead with what the user can now *do*, not the internal feature name.
