# Teach a commercial insight — don't recite features

The Challenger research (Gartner/CEB, 6,000 reps) found that in complex B2B, **~54% of star performers are
Challengers** — reps who *teach* the customer something new about their own business, *tailor* it to the
buyer, and *take control* of the conversation. Feature-recitation is what order-takers do; it loses to the
status quo because it gives the buyer no new reason to think differently.

## Teach — the commercial insight
- Bring **one true, non-obvious thing** about the buyer's problem they hadn't seen — a cost they're not
  counting, a risk hiding in the status quo, a benchmark against peers, a second-order consequence.
- The insight must **lead to you** — it reframes the problem in a way your strength uniquely solves. (Not
  generic thought-leadership; a *commercial* insight that connects to your value.)
- It should create *constructive tension*: "the way you're doing this is quietly costing you X" — earning
  the right to challenge, not just agree.

## Tailor
- Adapt the insight to *this* stakeholder's world and metric. The CFO hears margin; the ops lead hears
  hours; the VP hears risk. Same insight, tailored language (`know-the-buyer-cold`,
  `build-and-test-the-champion`).

## Take control
- Be comfortable with the parts that make "relationship builders" cringe: talk about **money**, press for
  **commitments**, and direct the process. Taking control is *not* being pushy — it's owning the buying
  journey so the buyer doesn't drift (it pairs with `close-with-a-mutual-action-plan`, and is the *good*
  kind of control — never the hard sell).

## The discipline
- Earn the right to teach with `know-the-buyer-cold` — an insight that's wrong or generic destroys
  credibility faster than no insight.
- Teaching ≠ lecturing. Deliver the insight, then ask — it's a conversation that opens discovery, not a
  monologue.
- Tension must be *constructive*. The goal is to make them think, then help — not to make them feel dumb.
- When the buyer asks for a feature bake-off, reframe to the *outcome* the features serve; don't win the
  spec sheet and lose the why.
