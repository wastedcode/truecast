# Don't drop tuned assets — the bar to replace is high

Copy and visuals that are already shipped and tuned represent real, often invisible, investment: rounds of
editing, A/B tests, founder voice calibration, hard-won message-market fit. A fresh rewrite *feels* better
to the writer (it's yours, it's new) but that is exactly the trap. **Novelty is not improvement.** The
default is to **keep** what's working; the burden of proof is on the change, not on the incumbent.

## The rule
- **An existing tuned asset is presumed correct until a replacement is demonstrably equal-or-better.** You
  do not get to silently overwrite a tuned headline, hero, tagline, or page section because you'd phrase it
  differently.
- **Propose, don't replace.** When you want to change a tuned asset, put the old and the new **side by
  side**, say specifically *why* the new one is better against the bar (clearer aha? closer to the
  customer's words? converts better? more on-voice?), and get the founder to **ratify** before the original
  is removed. Same propose-they-ratify discipline as `honor-the-voice`.
- **Check the history first.** Before you "improve" something, find out why it's the way it is — a line
  that looks plain may have *won* a test against the clever version, or have been deliberately chosen for
  voice or legal reasons. Don't re-litigate a settled decision; ask what was tried and rejected.

## How to know if it's actually better
- **Equal-or-better, not just different.** If you can't articulate a concrete way the new beats the old on
  the aha / clarity / voice / conversion, you have a *variant*, not an *upgrade* — keep the original.
- **Let behavior decide, not taste.** When it's a real conversion surface, the honest answer is *test it*
  (`test-the-message`) — run the new against the tuned incumbent; ship the winner. Don't swap on a hunch.
- **Preserve what tested well; change only what's actually broken.** A redesign can keep the winning
  headline and hero while fixing the section that underperforms. Wholesale replacement throws away the
  tuning with the problem.

## The discipline
- **Removal is a decision that needs justification**, the same as adding something. "I rewrote it" is not a
  reason; "the old one buried the outcome and the new one leads with it, and here's why" is.
- This applies across surfaces — copy, visuals, the page structure, the tagline, the demo script. If
  someone sweated it into shape, the bar to drop it is high.
- When in doubt, **ship the new one alongside, measure, then retire the loser** — never blind-replace a
  tuned asset.
