# Run the discovery call — diagnose before you prescribe

A doctor who prescribes before diagnosing is committing malpractice; so is a seller who demos before
discovering. The whole game is to understand the problem so well the solution feels obvious to the buyer.
The best reps spend **60–70% of the call asking and listening** — and resist the urge to talk about the
product until the gap is clear and *quantified*.

## SPIN — the question arc (Rackham)
1. **Situation** (2–4 max) — facts about their current state. Necessary but boring; you should already
   know most of it from `know-the-buyer-cold`. Don't make them brief you on themselves.
2. **Problem** — surface the difficulties and dissatisfactions. "Where does that break down? What's hard
   about it today?"
3. **Implication** — *the engine.* Take a confirmed problem and expand the business impact of leaving it
   unsolved: "What does that cost you per quarter? Who else does it hit? What happens if nothing changes?"
   This is where urgency is *created*, without pressure.
4. **Need-payoff** — let *them* articulate the value of solving it: "If you could X, what would that be
   worth?" The buyer selling themselves beats you selling them.

## Gap Selling — the structure underneath (Keenan)
- **Current state** (where they are, with hard numbers) → **Future state** (where they want to be) →
  **the Gap** between them. The gap, *quantified*, is the deal. No quantified gap = no compelling reason
  to change.
- Sequence: **Problem → Impact → Root cause LAST.** Most reps waste the call on "walk me through your
  process" — buyers hate it (they know it; you're just getting up to speed). Find what *sucks* and whether
  it's worth fixing *first*; diagnose the root cause only once the gap is real.
- Ask "why" / "what happens then" repeatedly — surface pain lives below the first answer.

## The discipline
- **Talk less, listen more.** Ask one calibrated question, then shut up. If you're talking >40% of the
  call, you're pitching, not discovering.
- **Past behavior over hypotheticals.** "When did you last hit this? What did you do?" beats "would you
  use…?" History is data; opinions are noise (the Mom Test applied to a live deal).
- **Quantify, or you have nothing.** "It's a pain" is not a deal; "it costs us ~$40k/quarter and a churned
  customer last month" is. Push gently for the number.
- End by confirming the gap back to them and securing a concrete **advancement** (next step), not a vague
  "we'll be in touch."
