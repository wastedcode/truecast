# Positioning & messaging foundations

The deep craft behind product marketing — the references the skills lean on. Read on demand.

## The one allegiance: the customer's understanding, not clever copy
- **Ries & Trout (*Positioning*)** — *"Positioning is not what you do to a product. It is what you do to
  the mind of the prospect."* Marketing is a battle for perception, not product superiority. Positioning is
  **context-setting**.
- **Dunford (*Obviously Awesome*)** — *"Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the
  best at something that a defined market cares a lot about."* And: *"customers use what they know to
  figure out what they don't"* — context (the category, the alternative) makes value legible.
- **Levitt / Christensen (JTBD)** — *"People don't want a quarter-inch drill; they want a quarter-inch
  hole."* Customers **hire** products to make progress. Functional + social + emotional jobs.
- The bar is the **aha moment**: a page/blog/email that leads straight to *"oh — I get it, I want this."*
  If it doesn't, the work isn't done. The right language is a **product decision**, not decoration.

## Dunford's positioning components (5+1, causal order)
1. **Competitive alternatives** — what they'd do without you (often the status quo / spreadsheet / intern).
2. **Unique attributes** — what you have that the alternatives lack.
3. **Value (+ proof)** — the benefit those attributes enable, that the target cares a lot about.
4. **Target who cares most** — the best-fit segment (work backwards from your differentiated value).
5. **Market category** — the frame that makes the value obvious; choosing it is the highest-leverage move.
6. **(+1) Relevant trends** — ride only true ones; a bolted-on trend reads as hype.
Positioning must be **true** — traceable to a shipped capability. Pre-users it's a *hypothesis* (analogy +
competitor language + founder intuition) — label it; test it on the first real users.

## The strategic narrative (Andy Raskin, *"The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen"*)
1. Name the **change in the world** (the undeniable shift / why-now) — not "you have a problem."
2. Show **winners and losers** (the stakes).
3. Tease the **Promised Land** — a future state, *not* your product.
4. Features enter as **"magic gifts"** for reaching it.
5. **Evidence** you can deliver — ideally a story of someone like them.
The Promised Land is defined in the customer's terms; if you can't describe it without your features, it's
a brochure, not a narrative.

## The messaging house
- **Core message** (one sentence) → **3–5 pillars** (themes the audience cares about) → **proof points**
  (the evidence under each). One page. Every artifact derives from it; it's the referee for "off-message."
- Map each pillar to a **job** and an **awareness stage**; re-weight pillars per segment (same core).

## Awareness & sophistication (Eugene Schwartz, *Breakthrough Advertising*, 1966)
- **Five stages of awareness:** unaware → problem-aware → solution-aware → product-aware → most-aware. Say
  only what they're ready to hear; match the headline/hook/CTA to the stage. Don't pitch the offer to the
  unaware.
- **Five stages of market sophistication:** as a market hears a claim repeatedly, the plain claim stops
  working → enlarge the claim → introduce a new **mechanism** ("why it works") → identify with the
  prospect. In a saturated category, a louder same-claim is invisible — change the mechanism or the frame.
- Schwartz's core law: you **channel existing desire**, you don't manufacture it.

## Voice & truth
- **Read the existing copy first** — the voice has a shape; honor it. **Preview** headline/lede/angle and
  get direction approved before drafting (it's the founder's voice — propose, they ratify).
- **Truth is a correctness gate:** copy must match what the product actually does. A claim it can't back
  reads as a lie — especially to a technical audience. No invented adjectives, no vaporware-as-shipped.
- **Curse of knowledge** is the enemy: the team is too close to read its own copy as a stranger does.
