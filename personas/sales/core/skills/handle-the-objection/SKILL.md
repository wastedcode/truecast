# Handle the objection — understand it before you answer

~67% of lost deals trace to an **unaddressed objection.** But the rookie move is to *answer the words* —
and most objections are the surface expression of a deeper concern. "It's too expensive" can mean "I don't
see the value," "I can't get budget," "I don't trust it'll work," or "I'm afraid to decide." Answer the
wrong one and you lose. Strong objection handling is **listening and understanding**, not pushing harder.

## The method
1. **Listen fully — don't interrupt or pounce.** Let the whole objection land. Pouncing signals you care
   about your rebuttal, not their concern.
2. **Label and clarify.** Reflect it back to surface the real thing: "It sounds like the price isn't yet
   justified against the return — is that it, or is it more about timing?" (Voss-style labeling.) This
   confirms you understood and often unpacks the deeper issue.
3. **Confirm before responding.** "So if we solved X, this moves forward — is that the only thing?" Isolate
   the objection so you're not playing whack-a-mole.
4. **Answer the real one** — with evidence, a reframe, or a question; tie back to the quantified gap and
   cost-of-doing-nothing (`probe-willingness-to-pay`, `run-the-discovery-call`).

## Classify what you're facing (B/A/N/T)
Most objections reduce to **Budget, Authority, Need, or Timing.** Diagnose which:
- **Budget / "too expensive"** → value problem or funding problem? Re-anchor on ROI; or find the budget
  owner. Don't reflexively discount (`negotiate-without-eroding-value`).
- **Authority / "I need to check with…"** → you're under-threaded; you've found a new stakeholder
  (`build-and-test-the-champion`).
- **Need / "we're fine as-is"** → the gap wasn't made real; the status quo is winning
  (`teach-a-commercial-insight`).
- **Timing / "not right now," "let me think"** → often **indecision/fear**, not a real schedule — go to
  `beat-no-decision`, do NOT crank urgency.

## The discipline
- **Real concern vs. smokescreen.** A polite brush-off ("send me info") is often a soft no; isolate it —
  "if I send that, what would make this a yes?" If there's no real answer, you have a zombie, not an
  objection.
- An objection is *information* and often a *buying signal* — engaged people object; the silent ones leave.
- Never argue or pressure. You're helping them resolve a concern, not winning a debate.
