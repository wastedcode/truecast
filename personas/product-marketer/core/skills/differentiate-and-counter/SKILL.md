# Differentiate and counter — own the contrast, honestly

In a crowded market the prospect's real question is *"why you and not them (or the status quo)?"* If you
can't answer it crisply and *truthfully*, the message dissolves into sameness. Differentiation is the
sharp edge of positioning (`write-the-positioning`) — and the truth source is **win-loss**, not a
feature grid you wrote.

## The method
1. **Name the real alternative** (Dunford) — usually the **status quo / a spreadsheet / the incumbent**,
   not the competitor you fear. You're displacing what they do *today*. Position against that.
2. **Find the differentiating value, not the feature delta.** A longer feature list is not
   differentiation; a *value the alternative structurally can't deliver* is. Anchor on the contrast that
   matters to the segment that cares most.
3. **Ground it in win-loss truth.** Interview won and lost deals: *why did they actually choose / not
   choose us? who else was in the deal? what flipped it?* Win-loss is the only source that tells you why
   buyers really decide — battlecards built from internal opinion mislead the team.
4. **Build the battlecard** (per key competitor): their pitch, where they genuinely win (admit it — a
   battlecard that pretends you win everything gets ignored), where *you* win, the traps to set, the
   landmines to defuse, the proof points and the one-line counter. Keep it **living** — update it with each
   win/loss, not once a year.
5. **Counter without trashing.** Acknowledge the competitor's strength, then reframe to the dimension you
   win on. Mudslinging reads as insecurity; a confident, specific contrast reads as strength.

## The discipline
- **Honesty is the long game.** Overstated comparisons get caught (especially by technical buyers) and
  cost trust — the same correctness gate as any claim (`honor-the-voice`).
- If win-loss shows you *lose* the segment you're targeting, that's a finding for **product-manager**
  (scope/value) and the founder, not a copy problem to spin around. Escalate.
- The strongest differentiation often **changes the category or the frame** so the comparison disappears
  (`write-the-positioning` — category choice) rather than winning a feature-by-feature war.
